# CIS111: Ethics in Information Technology

Technology gives people new power. Ethics helps you decide how that
power should be used. This book gives you practical ways to reason
through decisions about data, software, privacy, security, artificial
intelligence, and professional responsibility.

## About This Textbook

This textbook serves community college students in an introductory IT
ethics course. It assumes no prior ethics or IT coursework. Each chapter
defines the legal, technical, and ethical ideas you need before asking
you to apply them.

The 12 chapters stand on their own. Your instructor may assign them in
different combinations or at different speeds. The textbook does not
assume a specific term length.

## Course Learning Outcomes

By the end of CIS111, you will be able to:

* **CLO I.** Compare a variety of different ethical theories.
* **CLO II.** Analyze professional business ethics and the complexities
  of making ethical judgments.
* **CLO III.** Examine issues relating to corporate ownership, corporate
  governance, and responsiveness to corporate stakeholders.
* **CLO IV.** Evaluate how social media responsibility encompasses
  economic, legal, ethical, and privacy issues.
* **CLO V.** Illustrate whistle-blowing and protection for
  whistle-blowers.
* **CLO VI.** Analyze the major ethical challenges of operating in a
  healthcare environment using mobile and wireless technologies.
* **CLO VII.** Interpret fiduciary responsibilities of private
  information.
* **CLO VIII.** Examine issues with software and software liability.
* **CLO IX.** Apply cost/benefit analysis in information technology
  ethical decision-making.
* **CLO X.** Evaluate the influence of government regulations as well as
  professional self-regulation and sanctions in ethical information
  technology decision-making.
* **CLO XI.** Interpret intellectual property, copyright laws, and fair
  use doctrine.
* **CLO XII.** Classify hate speech and defamation.
* **CLO XIII.** Assess contemporary issues related to workplace
  monitoring.
* **CLO XIV.** Analyze the major identity theft issues.
* **CLO XV.** Evaluate Information Technology security breaches and risk
  assessments.
* **CLO XVI.** Formulate security policies within Information
  Technology.
* **CLO XVII.** Critique professional codes of ethics for Information
  Technology professionals.
* **CLO XVIII.** Develop prevention, detection, and responses to
  Information Technology security attacks.

## How This Textbook Is Organized

### Part I: Foundations of Ethics (Chapters 1-3)

You build a practical ethical reasoning toolkit. You then apply it to
professional conduct, corporate responsibility, and AI governance.

### Part II: Digital Rights and Responsibilities (Chapters 4-6)

You examine how organizations handle social media, whistleblowing, and
health information. Each chapter asks you to balance competing rights
and duties.

### Part III: Legal Frameworks and IT Obligations (Chapters 7-9)

You connect ethical reasoning to software liability, regulation, and
intellectual property. The focus shifts from identifying issues to
defending decisions.

### Part IV: Security, Privacy, and Professional Practice (Chapters 10-12)

You assess speech, monitoring, identity theft, and cybersecurity. The
book closes with professional codes and a capstone ethical analysis.
