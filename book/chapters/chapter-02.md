In 2015, US regulators disclosed that Volkswagen had used software to detect emissions tests. The code changed how some diesel engines behaved during a test. On the road, those vehicles could emit far more nitrogen oxides than the legal limit. The scheme required people inside a large firm to build, approve, or overlook deceptive software. It shows how workplace pressure can turn a tech task into a moral choice.

This chapter centers on a hard question: what should you do when your firm asks for something you believe is wrong? IT workers have duties to firms, clients, vendors, peers, users, and the public. Those duties can conflict. You need a sound process before that happens.

## 2.1 Professional Ethics vs. Personal Ethics

In Chapter 1, you learned that morals are your own beliefs, ethics provides shared tools for group thinking, and laws set the legal minimum. This chapter asks a more focused question: what happens when your personal ethics meet the duties of your job?

**Personal ethics** are the principles you live by in your everyday life. They come from your upbringing, your culture, your religion, and your experiences. You might believe that honesty is always the best policy, or that loyalty to friends matters above everything else. These beliefs guide your own choices.

**Professional ethics** are standards of conduct tied to a field of work. They go beyond your own beliefs. People in a field share duties such as guarding data, stating system limits honestly, and disclosing conflicts of interest. Groups such as ACM and IEEE publish formal codes that describe these duties.

Here is the main difference: personal ethics belong to you, while professional ethics belong to the role. A database admin, network engineer, or software developer accepts the duties tied to that job. Those duties apply even when they conflict with the worker's beliefs.

Sometimes personal and professional ethics align perfectly. You personally believe in honesty, and your field needs transparent communication with clients. No conflict there. But sometimes they clash. Your firm asks you to implement a feature you believe is deceptive. Your personal ethics say refuse. Your work duty to your firm says comply. That gap between personal conviction and professional expectation is where the hardest choices happen.

### How Professional Ethics Differ from Personal Ethics

The following table shows the main differences.

| | **Personal Ethics** | **Professional Ethics** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Source** | Family, culture, religion, life experience | Professional codes, firm policies, industry standards |
| **Scope** | Guides your private choices | Governs your conduct in a work role |
| **Accountability** | Accountable to yourself and your community | Accountable to firms, clients, colleagues, and the field |
| **Flexibility** | You decide what matters most to you | The field sets shared minimum standards |
| **When they conflict** | You may need to decide whether to comply or push back | Violating professional ethics can result in termination, legal action, or loss of certification |

This distinction prevents two mistakes. Your own morals may not cover a new IT case. Field standards also do not excuse harm. If a job duty calls for a harmful act, you may need to question or refuse it.

**Worked role check:** A client asks a support worker to email a full
user list to a personal account. The worker wants to help fast. Their
role, though, requires them to guard user data and use approved tools.

The worker can state the conflict in plain terms. The client needs a
file. The requested path can expose people and break the data rule. The
worker should offer a safe share link or ask the data owner to approve a
narrow export.

This is not a choice between being kind and doing the job. A sound work
role includes helping the client without placing other people at risk.
The safer path serves both duties.

A second worker faces a new case. Their friend asks them to reset an
account with no ID check. The worker trusts the friend, but the support
role requires the same check for each person. The worker can help the
friend use the normal reset path. Fair process protects the account and
the friendship. It also gives the team a clean log if the reset is
questioned later.

The same fair rule guards trust and the user. It also helps the team act
the same way when the next request arrives.

Clear rules help. Good logs show why the worker said no. That record
helps the team learn.

## 2.2 The Six Work Relationships for IT Workers

IT workers do not work in isolation. Each day, you interact with people who depend on your tech knowledge, honesty, and judgment. These interactions form six work relationships. Each carries its own duties and risks.

### Tie 1: IT Workers and Firms

Your firm pays your salary and provides the tools for you to do your work. In return, you owe your firm loyalty, competence, and honest effort. But that loyalty has limits.

**Acceptable use policies (AUPs)** define how staff can use firm tech. Most groups prohibit outside business, unauthorized software, and improper access on work devices. Breaking an AUP can lead to discipline or job loss.

**Conflicts of interest** occur when your own interests interfere with your work judgment. If you are responsible for selecting a new software vendor and your brother-in-law owns one of the firms bidding for the contract, you have a conflict of interest. The duty is to disclose the conflict, not to pretend it does not exist.

**Trade secrets** are private business data that gives a firm an edge. Examples include client lists, source code, prices, and internal methods. When you leave a firm, you have a moral and often legal duty not to take them. This applies even if you created the work while employed there.

### Tie 2: IT Workers and Clients

When you work with clients, you owe them honest claims and confidentiality. If a system cannot handle 10,000 users at once, you must say so. A false claim made to close a deal is a breach of trust.

**Confidentiality** means guarding data that clients share with you. A database consultant may see client records, financial data, and business plans. Using that data for personal gain, or sharing it with another client, breaks trust.

### Tie 3: IT Workers and Suppliers

Vendors and suppliers want your business. They may offer gifts, free trips, or other incentives to shape purchasing choices. A free pen at a trade show is harmless. An all-expenses-paid vacation from a vendor whose contract you are evaluating is not.

Use a simple test for gifts: could this gift shape my judgment or appear to do so? If yes, decline it or disclose it. Many firms set a dollar limit for vendor gifts.

### Tie 4: IT Workers and Other Professionals

Your duties to colleagues in the field include honesty, fair competition, and mutual respect. Two issues stand out in IT.

**Resume fraud** is the practice of exaggerating or fabricating qualifications on a resume. Claiming certifications you do not hold, inflating job titles, or inventing work experience are all forms of resume fraud. In IT, where certifications like CompTIA, CISSP, and AWS carry real weight, resume fraud is not just dishonest. It puts groups at risk by placing unqualified people in tech roles.

**Whistleblowing within the field** occurs when an IT worker reports wrong or illegal conduct by a peer. Examples include improper system access or skipped safety tests. Chapter 5 covers the topic in depth. For now, know that a duty to speak up may outweigh loyalty to a coworker.

### Tie 5: IT Workers and Users

If you build, maintain, or manage tech systems, you have a direct duty to the people who use them. This means guarding user data from unauthorized access, communicating honestly about what a system does and does not do, and designing systems that do not mislead or manipulate.

Consider a developer who designs a subscription cancellation process. Hiding the cancel option behind confusing screens is a **dark pattern**. It may reduce cancellations, but it exploits user trust. Your duty to users includes making choices clear, even when the firm benefits from confusion.

### Tie 6: IT Workers and Society

Your broadest duty is to the public. IT workers shape systems that millions of people depend on. That power creates a duty to avoid harm.

**Environmental impact** is one part of that duty. Data centers use large amounts of energy. You may not control your firm's climate plan, but you can support efficient systems and question waste that has no clear gain.

**The digital divide** creates another public duty. Not everyone has equal access to tech. A system that needs fast internet, costly devices, or advanced skills may exclude people. Treat access and inclusion as design needs, not afterthoughts.

## 2.3 Duties of IT Users

So far, this chapter has focused on IT workers, people who build, manage, and maintain tech systems. But duties do not belong only to workers. Each person who uses tech in a group has duties too.

**IT users** are staff, contractors, students, and anyone else who uses a group's tech tools. You do not need to be an engineer to have duties. If you use a firm laptop, connect to a firm network, or access a shared database, you are an IT user with duties.

### Acceptable Use and Resource Stewardship

Most groups need staff to sign an **acceptable use policy (AUP)** before granting access to tech tools. These policies often ban unauthorized software, personal business, password sharing, and access beyond a person's role.

Following an AUP is not just about avoiding punishment. It is about being a responsible member of a group. When one worker installs unauthorized software that introduces malware, the entire network is at risk. When someone shares a password, each person who uses that password becomes a security vulnerability. Individual carelessness creates collective harm.

### Software Piracy and Licensing Compliance

Using unlicensed software on a work computer is illegal and wrong. It violates copyright, creates legal risk, and harms the people who made the software. This applies even when someone installs a personal copy only to avoid buying another license.

### Protecting Shared Data

IT users often have access to data that does not belong to them. A human resources assistant can see salary data. A client service representative can see billing records. An intern may see private project files. The rule is clear: access only the data your job needs, and never use workplace data for own gain.

## 2.4 Building an Ethical IT Group

Individual ethics matter, but they are not enough. Groups shape actions. A firm that rewards cutting corners will produce staff who cut corners, no matter how strong their own values are. This section examines what groups can do to encourage sound conduct among IT workers and users alike.

### Setting the Ethical Tone

The strongest force in workplace ethics is leaders' conduct. When leaders follow standards in public and in private, staff take the rules seriously. When leaders bend rules or ignore harm, staff learn that ethics are optional. This signal is called **tone at the top**.

### Creating Effective Codes of Conduct

A **code of conduct** states how people in a group should act. A code of ethics gives broad principles. A code of conduct gives rules for gifts, conflicts of interest, and data handling.

A sound code has three traits. First, its rules are clear enough to use. Second, leaders enforce it fairly. Third, the group reviews it as tech and risk change. A code that binds junior staff but excuses leaders has little value.

### Ethics Training and Reporting Systems

Codes work only when staff know and trust them. **Ethics training** helps staff spot a concern early. Strong training uses cases, discussion, and practice instead of a checklist alone.

**Reporting systems** give staff a safe way to raise concerns. Examples include an anonymous hotline and a formal complaint path. If staff fear retaliation, they will stay silent. Leaders must protect good-faith reports and act on valid claims.

### The Complexity of Choice-Making in Business

Even with strong workplace support, choices in business are rarely simple. Here is why.

**Stakeholders want different things.** A choice that helps shareholders may harm staff. A feature that raises engagement may weaken privacy. Few options satisfy everyone.

**Time pressure limits thought.** You may have minutes, not days, to act. Practice with ethics frameworks now so you can use them under pressure.

**Workplace culture creates pressure to conform.** Speaking up is hard when people around you accept a harmful act. The Volkswagen case shows how a large workplace can enable deception. People may approve, build, or overlook a system because compliance feels safer than resistance.

**Ambiguity is common.** Many cases in IT do not have a clear "right" answer. Reasonable people, applying the same ethical frameworks, can reach different conclusions. That does not mean ethics is useless. It means ethical thinking is a practice, not a formula.

### AI Tools and Work Accountability

AI tools screen applicants, approve transactions, moderate content, and flag threats. Your duties do not move to the tool. The [ACM Code of Ethics](https://www.acm.org/about-acm/code-of-ethics) asks members to avoid harm, state system limits, and assess risk. The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) adds a process for governing and measuring AI risk.

This changes the supplier tie from Section 2.2. Price and support still matter. You must also ask whether the vendor documents training data, permits testing, reports known limits, and supports meaningful human review. A contract promise cannot replace facts.

You may be asked to maintain an AI system you did not build and cannot fully inspect. Ask what assumptions shape its outputs, where it has failed, and who can stop or correct a harmful choice. Those questions protect users, clients, firms, and the public.

## 2.5 Summary and Retrieval 💡

### Key Concepts

* **Professional ethics differ from personal ethics.** Personal ethics come from your beliefs and life. Professional ethics are shared standards tied to a specific role. IT duties include guarding data, disclosing conflicts, and stating system limits honestly.

* **IT workers manage six work relationships.** Firms, clients, suppliers, other IT workers, users, and the public each create distinct duties. Knowing these groups helps you spot risk before harm occurs.

* **IT users have duties too.** They must follow acceptable use policies, respect software licenses, and protect shared data.

* **Groups shape conduct.** Leaders' examples, codes, training, and safe reporting systems show whether ethics matter. Personal integrity may not overcome a workplace that rewards harm.

* **Choices at work are complex.** Stakeholder conflict, time pressure, workplace culture, and uncertainty can pull you in several directions. Practicing a sound process before a crisis helps.
