## 3.1 Shareholders, Stakeholders, and Corporate Governance

When a firm faces an ethical choice, whose interests come first? Two theories offer different answers.

### Shareholder Theory

**Shareholder theory** says a corporation's main duty is to earn profits for its owners. Economist Milton Friedman made a famous case for this view in 1970. He argued that a firm should raise profits while obeying the rules. In this view, leaders should not spend firm money on social goals without the owners' consent.

In the tech industry, shareholder theory shows up whenever a firm prioritizes revenue growth, stock price, or quarterly earnings above other concerns. When a social media platform delays safety features because they would reduce user engagement, that choice reflects shareholder-first thinking.

### Stakeholder Theory

**Stakeholder theory** takes a broader view. It says a corporation has duties to everyone affected by its work, not just shareholders. R. Edward Freeman developed this framework in 1984. A **stakeholder** is any person or group that can affect, or is affected by, a firm's actions.

For a tech firm, stakeholders typically include:

* **Shareholders and investors** who provide capital and expect returns
* **Staff** who depend on the firm for income and working conditions
* **Customers and users** who rely on the firm's products and services
* **Communities** affected by the firm's work, hiring, and climate impact
* **Suppliers and contractors** such as gig workers and outsourced labor
* **Government and regulators** who set the legal framework for business conduct

This view links long-term success to sound ties with all these groups. It does not put short-term gains for one group first.

### Corporate Governance: Who Decides?

**Corporate governance** is the system used to direct and control a firm. At most large firms, the key players include:

* **The board of directors**, which oversees the firm's plan and holds leaders accountable
* **Executive leaders** (CEO, CTO, CFO), who make day-to-day choices
* **Shareholders**, who elect the board and vote on major choices

The board has an ethical role that goes beyond financial review. It sets the tone for the whole firm. When a board values ethics, that focus flows downward. When a board ignores harm in favor of growth, staff get the message.

In tech firms, governance choices carry added weight because the products affect so many people. A board may rush a product, cut a safety team, or ignore known bias. Each act can affect millions of people.

## 3.2 Corporate Social Responsibility in Tech

**Corporate social responsibility (CSR)** means that businesses have duties to society beyond profit. CSR includes fair treatment of staff, community support, ethical conduct, and efforts to reduce harm.

### Why CSR Matters for Tech Firms

Tech firms face high CSR expectations because their products shape daily life. One harmful choice can reach millions of people within hours. CSR gives firms several ways to reduce that risk.

**Gaining community goodwill.** Firms that support education and act as responsible neighbors can build trust. That trust can aid hiring, sales, and partnerships. When a firm loses public trust, it may take years to rebuild.

**Creating consistent organizational behavior.** A strong CSR plan gives staff a guide for hard choices. Clear values tell people what the firm expects. Without that guide, staff may handle the same risk in different ways.

**Fostering good business practices.** Firms can record key choices, audit systems, and set goals. These habits support ethics and sound work.

**Protecting the firm from legal action.** A firm can address harm before it leads to a lawsuit or state action. Early action may save money and protect trust.

**Avoiding unfavorable publicity.** One ethical failure can become a global news story within hours. A strong CSR plan can help prevent a failure or guide the firm's response.

### The Gig Economy and Outsourcing Ethics

CSR becomes harder when firms rely on workers who are not staff. **Contingent workers**, such as gig workers, freelancers, and contractors, often lack the benefits and job security that full-time staff receive.

Consider a ride-sharing firm that treats its drivers as contractors. The drivers do not receive health insurance, paid time off, or workers' compensation. The firm pays less for labor. Do those drivers still count as stakeholders? Stakeholder theory says yes. CSR extends the firm's ethical duties beyond its payroll.

Outsourcing raises similar concerns. A tech firm may gain from low-cost overseas labor while workers face harm without enough support. Does the contracting firm share a duty to those workers even when another firm employs them?

## 3.3 Building Ethical Organizations

Knowing that CSR matters is one thing. Building an ethical organization is another. This section covers tools that turn stated values into conduct.

### Appointing a Chief Ethics Officer

Some groups appoint a **Chief Ethics Officer** or a similar leader. This person may report to the CEO or board. The role includes finding risks, setting rules, reviewing complaints, and helping staff with hard choices.

An ethics officer signals that ethics is a leadership concern. The role works only when the officer has real authority and independence. An officer who can be punished for raising a concern offers little protection.

### Establishing a Code of Ethics

A **code of ethics** is a written guide to the ethical standards a group expects from its staff. A good code does more than list rules. It links those rules to shared values and gives help when no rule fits.

Sound codes of ethics share several features:

* They are written in clear, accessible language that all staff can understand
* They address the ethical challenges the group is most likely to face
* They include examples and cases, not just abstract principles
* They show how to report ethical concerns and protect people who speak up
* They are reviewed and updated regularly as the group and its industry change

A code that sits unread in a handbook has little value. The firm must use it in training, work reviews, and daily choices.

### Conducting Social Audits

A **social audit** reviews a firm's impact on its stakeholders and communities. It tests the firm's work against its stated values and CSR goals. The audit may cover climate impact, labor, community ties, and supply chain ethics.

The key gain of a social audit is accountability. It makes the firm test its conduct against clear criteria instead of vague promises.

### Ethics Training and Performance Appraisals

Training turns ethical rules into staff conduct. Sound ethics training is not a one-time event. It uses realistic cases and gives staff practice before a high-stakes choice occurs.

Some groups add ethical criteria to performance appraisals. Staff are judged on how they reached a result, not just the result. Promotions and raises can then reward integrity as well as output.

## 3.4 AI Ethics: Governance, Accountability, and Global Approaches

This section is the AI ethics anchor for the book. You will use its five tests in later chapters.

### Why AI Ethics Is a Corporate Governance Issue

AI systems affect jobs, loans, health care, courts, and online posts. When a tool causes harm, leaders must answer. Who chose it? Who tested it? Who watched it? Who can stop it?

A board may demand a fast launch but refuse to fund safety tests. A CEO may form a review team but give it no power. Both are leadership choices.

### Core AI Ethics Principles

Five principles guide sound AI work. You will use them throughout this book.

**Fairness.** An AI tool should not put one group at an unfair loss based on race, sex, age, disability, or another protected trait.

**Transparency.** A group should state how an AI tool works, what data it uses, and how it shapes a choice. People should know when AI has a role.

**Accountability.** Named people and groups must answer for an AI tool and any harm it causes.

**Explainability.** A person harmed by an AI choice should get a reason they can grasp and challenge.

**Human oversight.** People should review high-stakes AI choices and have the power to change them.

### Algorithmic Bias: How It Happens and Who It Harms

**Algorithmic bias** occurs when an AI tool produces unfair results in a set pattern. Bias can enter at three main points.

**Biased training data.** AI systems learn from past data. If prior hiring favored men, a tool trained on those records may repeat the bias.

**Biased design choices.** A team picks the data, the goal, and the test for success. Those choices can reflect the team's blind spots. A team may miss harm to people whose lives differ from its own.

**Biased use context.** A sound tool can fail when used for the wrong task or group. For example, a face match tool may work less well for skin tones that were scarce in its test data.

This harm often falls on groups that already face unfair barriers. It can block a job, loan, health service, or fair court result.

### Global AI Approaches

AI rules differ by firm and place. Yet AI tools cross borders. A model built in one nation may be used in many more.

**Firm practices vary.** One firm may share a **model card**, a short report on a model's use, tests, and limits. It may also share audit notes. Another firm may keep those facts private. A useful rule must change how teams build, test, launch, watch, and fix a tool.

**National frameworks differ.** The [EU AI Act](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai) sets rules by risk level. As of July 2026, its bans, AI literacy rules, and general-purpose AI rules apply. Other parts start later. In the United States, the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) is voluntary. Its four main steps are Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage. NIST is revising version 1.0.

**Global principles aid comparison.** The [OECD AI Principles](https://oecd.ai/en/ai-principles) address human rights, transparency, safety, and accountability. They were updated in 2024. No set of rules can replace facts about the tool and the people it may harm.

The [IEEE Ethically Aligned Design guide](https://standards.ieee.org/ieee/White_Paper/10594/) asks teams to place human well-being and shared values at the center of design. It is a design guide, not a law.

### Who Is Accountable When AI Causes Harm?

Consider a hospital AI tool that fails to detect cancer. The delay harms the patient. Who is responsible?

* The firm that built the AI tool?
* The hospital that chose to use it?
* The doctor who relied on its advice?
* The regulators who approved it (or failed to regulate it)?

An **accountability gap** forms when many parties share a tool but no one owns the result. Before use, a group should name who watches the tool, fixes errors, and helps people who face harm.

Clear roles close that gap. Leaders should name who approves the tool, takes complaints, and can pause its use. The [NIST AI RMF Core](https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/) makes Govern the base for Map, Measure, and Manage.

### The Direct-Verify-Intervene Practice

The **direct-verify-intervene practice** gives AI users three duties. Direct the tool toward a clear goal. Verify its results over time. Intervene when a result cannot be explained or defended. The five AI ethics principles turn those duties into tests a team can use.

You may have to care for a tool you did not build. Ask what shaped it, where it failed, who checks high-stakes results, and who can stop it. Ask before use, not after harm.

### Green Computing and the Climate Ethics of AI

AI governance is not just about fairness and accountability. It also includes climate duty. Training and running large AI models can use much power and water. Firms should track those costs and weigh them before choosing a system.

**Green computing** means making, using, and discarding tech in ways that reduce harm. For AI, it asks whether a larger model is worth its climate cost. It also asks firms to track and report the resources their tools use.

## 3.5 Summary and Retrieval 💡

### Key Concepts

* **Shareholder theory and stakeholder theory** give different views of firm duty. One centers the owners. The other includes everyone affected by the firm's work.

* **Corporate governance** sets who makes choices and who answers for them. In tech firms, these choices can affect millions of people.

* **Corporate social responsibility (CSR)** means that businesses have duties beyond profit. Ethics officers, codes, audits, training, and fair work reviews help a firm act on those duties.

* **AI ethics is a corporate governance issue.** Its core principles are fairness, transparency, accountability, explainability, and human oversight. Bias can enter through data, design, or use.

* **Global AI approaches** differ. The EU AI Act sets legal rules by risk. The US NIST AI Risk Management Framework gives voluntary guidance.

* **AI accountability needs named people and facts.** A group must state who approves a tool, watches it, takes complaints, and can stop it.

### Case: RecruitIQ and the Bias Discovery

TalentForward is a tech firm with 2,000 staff. Two years ago, it bought an AI hiring tool named RecruitIQ. The tool scans resumes, ranks people, and picks who moves to an interview. The firm hoped to save time and reduce human bias.

Last month, the firm's new Chief Ethics Officer, Dr. Amara Osei, audited the tool. She found four risks:

* RecruitIQ ranked graduates of historically Black colleges and universities (HBCUs) below other graduates with the same skills.
* The tool lowered scores for work gaps. This had a greater effect on women who took parental leave.
* The vendor would not share the source code. An outside audit was not possible.

Dr. Osei gave the findings to the CEO and board. The CEO agreed that the bias was a problem. Yet RecruitIQ had cut hiring costs by 30%. The contract had one year left, and an early end would cost $500,000. Some board members wanted to keep the tool. Others saw a risk of bias claims and lost trust.
