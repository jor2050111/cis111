## 3.1 Shareholders, Stakeholders, and Corporate Governance

When a firm makes an ethical choice, the first question is: whose interests should it prioritize? Two competing theories offer highly different answers.

### Shareholder Theory

**Shareholder theory** argues that a corporation's primary obligation is to maximize profits for its owners (shareholders). The economist Milton Friedman made the most famous case for this position in 1970. He argued that the only social responsibility of a business is to increase its profits, as long as it stays within the rules of the game. In this view, executives who spend firm money on social causes are essentially spending someone else's money without their permission.

In the technology industry, shareholder theory shows up whenever a firm prioritizes revenue growth, stock price, or quarterly earnings above other concerns. When a social media platform delays safety features because they would reduce user engagement, that choice reflects shareholder-first thinking.

### Stakeholder Theory

**Stakeholder theory** takes a broader view. It argues that a corporation has obligations to everyone affected by its operations, not just shareholders. R. Edward Freeman developed this framework in 1984. A **stakeholder** is any person or group that can affect, or is affected by, a firm's actions.

For a technology firm, stakeholders typically include:

* **Shareholders and investors** who provide capital and expect returns
* **Staff** who depend on the firm for income and working conditions
* **Customers and users** who rely on the firm's products and services
* **Communities** affected by the firm's operations, hiring, and environmental impact
* **Suppliers and contractors** such as gig workers and outsourced labor
* **Government and regulators** who set the legal framework for business conduct

Stakeholder theory says that long-term business success depends on maintaining healthy relationships with all of these groups, not just maximizing short-term returns for one of them.

### Corporate Governance: Who Decides?

**Corporate governance** is the system of rules, practices, and processes by which a firm is directed and controlled. At most large firms, the key players include:

* **The board of directors**, which oversees the firm's strategy and holds executive leadership accountable
* **Executive leadership** (CEO, CTO, CFO), which makes day-to-day choices
* **Shareholders**, who elect the board and vote on major choices

The board has an ethical role that goes beyond financial oversight. It sets the tone for the entire group. When a board prioritizes ethics, that priority flows downward. When a board ignores ethical concerns in favor of growth, staff get the message.

In technology firms, governance choices carry unusual weight because the products affect so many people. A board's choice to rush a product to market, cut a safety team, or delay addressing a known bias in an algorithm is a governance choice with ethical effects for millions.

## 3.2 Corporate Social Responsibility in Technology

**Corporate social responsibility (CSR)** is the idea that businesses have obligations to society that go beyond making money. CSR encompasses a firm's efforts to act ethically, contribute to the community, treat staff fairly, and minimize harm.

### Why CSR Matters for Tech Firms

Technology firms face especially high CSR expectations because their products are so deeply woven into daily life. When a tech firm behaves irresponsibly, the effects can scale to billions of people in a matter of hours. There are several practical reasons why CSR matters in the technology sector.

**Gaining community goodwill.** Firms that invest in their communities, support education, and act as responsible neighbors build trust. That trust translates into customer loyalty, better recruiting, and stronger partnerships. When a firm loses community trust, as Facebook did after the Cambridge Analytica scandal in 2018, rebuilding it takes years.

**Creating consistent organizational behavior.** A strong CSR commitment gives staff at every level a framework for making choices. When the firm's values are clear, people do not have to guess what is expected of them. Without that framework, individual staff are left to make ethical judgments on their own, and the results are inconsistent.

**Fostering good business practices.** Firms that take CSR seriously tend to adopt better internal processes. They document their choices, audit their systems, and set measurable goals. These habits improve not just ethics but overall operational quality.

**Protecting the group from legal action.** Proactive CSR efforts can reduce legal risk. Firms that address potential harms before they become lawsuits or regulatory actions save money and protect their reputations.

**Avoiding unfavorable publicity.** In the age of social media, a single ethical failure can become a global news story within hours. Firms with strong CSR programs are better positioned to prevent those failures and to respond effectively when problems do arise.

### The Gig Economy and Outsourcing Ethics

CSR becomes more complicated when firms rely on workers who are not technically staff. **Contingent workers**, such as gig workers, freelancers, and outsourced contractors, often lack the benefits, protections, and job security that full-time staff receive.

Consider a ride-sharing firm that classifies its drivers as independent contractors instead of staff. The drivers do not receive health insurance, paid time off, or workers' compensation. The firm benefits from lower labor costs. But do those drivers count as stakeholders? Stakeholder theory says yes. CSR frameworks suggest that a firm's ethical obligations extend to everyone who contributes to its success, not just those on the official payroll.

Outsourcing raises similar concerns. When a major technology firm contracts with overseas workers to moderate disturbing content at low wages, the firm benefits from cheaper labor, but the workers may face psychological harm without adequate support. The question is whether the contracting firm bears ethical responsibility for how those workers are treated, even if a third-party staffing agency technically employs them.

### Case Study 3.1 - The Content Moderator Crisis 📋

**The Situation:** GlobalTech, a major social media firm, contracts with Apex Staffing to hire content moderators in Nairobi, Kenya. The moderators review graphic content, such as violence, child exploitation, and hate speech, for about $2 per hour. After an investigative report reveals widespread psychological trauma among the moderators, several file a lawsuit alleging inadequate mental health support and exploitative working conditions.

GlobalTech responds by stating that the moderators are staff of Apex Staffing, not of GlobalTech, and that Apex is responsible for worker welfare. Apex Staffing argues that it follows local labor laws and that GlobalTech's content volume and speed requirements make it impossible to provide longer review times or additional breaks.

* **Content moderators** who face psychological harm from reviewing graphic material at high speed for low pay

* **Regulators and lawmakers** who are evaluating whether platform firms should be held responsible for the welfare of contracted workers

## 3.3 Building Ethical Groups

Knowing that CSR matters is one thing. Building a group that behaves ethically is another. This section covers the practical tools firms use to move from good intentions to consistent ethical behavior.

### Appointing a Chief Ethics Officer

Some groups appoint a **Chief Ethics Officer** or a similar executive-level role responsible for overseeing the firm's ethical conduct. This person typically reports directly to the CEO or the board of directors. Their job is to identify ethical risks, develop policies, investigate complaints, and serve as a resource for staff facing ethical dilemmas.

Having a dedicated ethics officer sends a signal throughout the group: ethics is not an afterthought. It is a leadership priority. However, the role is only effective if the officer has real authority and independence. An ethics officer who can be overruled or fired for raising uncomfortable concerns provides little actual protection.

### Establishing a Code of Ethics

A **code of ethics** is a written document that outlines the ethical standards a group expects from its staff. A good code of ethics does more than list rules. It describes the values behind those rules and provides guidance for situations the rules do not explicitly cover.

Effective codes of ethics share several features:

* They are written in clear, accessible language that all staff can understand
* They address the specific ethical challenges the group is most likely to face
* They include examples and cases, not just abstract principles
* They describe how to report ethical concerns and what protections exist for people who report them
* They are reviewed and updated regularly as the group and its industry change

A code of ethics that sits in a worker handbook and is never discussed is just a document. To matter, it must be woven into training, performance reviews, and everyday conversations.

### Conducting Social Audits

A **social audit** is a formal review of a firm's impact on its stakeholders and communities. It evaluates how well the firm is living up to its stated values and CSR commitments. Social audits can cover environmental impact, labor practices, community engagement, supply chain ethics, and more.

The key benefit of a social audit is accountability. It forces the firm to measure its ethical performance against specific criteria, instead of relying on vague claims about being "committed to doing the right thing."

### Ethics Training and Performance Appraisals

Training is how groups translate ethical policies into worker behavior. Effective ethics training is not a one-time event. It happens regularly, uses realistic cases, and gives staff practice making ethical choices in low-stakes settings so they are better prepared for high-stakes situations.

Some groups go further by such as **ethical criteria in performance appraisals**. This means that staff are evaluated not just on what they achieved but on how they achieved it. When ethical behavior is part of how promotions and raises are determined, staff have a concrete incentive to act with integrity.

## 3.4 AI Ethics: Governance, Accountability, and Global Approaches

This section is the AI ethics anchor for the entire course. The principles introduced here will reappear in every chapter that follows. Take time with this material. It forms the foundation for how you will think about AI throughout the rest of CIS111.

### Why AI Ethics Is a Corporate Governance Issue

Artificial intelligence systems make choices that affect hiring, lending, healthcare, criminal justice, content moderation, and countless other areas. When those systems cause harm, the question of accountability becomes a governance question: who inside the group approved the system? Who tested it? Who monitored its performance after deployment? And who is responsible when something goes wrong?

AI governance is not just a technical problem. It is a leadership problem. The choices firms make about how to develop, test, deploy, and monitor AI systems reflect the same governance structures you learned about in Section 3.1. A board that demands fast AI deployment without investing in safety testing is making a governance choice. A CEO who appoints an AI ethics team but gives it no real authority is making a governance choice. These choices have effects.

### Core AI Ethics Principles

Several principles have emerged as foundations for responsible AI development. You will see these terms throughout the rest of this book.

**Fairness.** AI systems should treat all people equitably. They should not produce outcomes that systematically disadvantage one group over another based on race, gender, age, disability, or other protected characteristics.

**Transparency.** Groups should be open about how their AI systems work, what data they use, and how choices are made. Users and affected communities deserve to know when AI is being used and how it affects them.

**Accountability.** There must be clear lines of responsibility for AI systems. When an AI system causes harm, specific people and groups must be answerable for that harm.

**Explainability.** AI systems should be able to explain their choices in terms that humans can understand. A hiring tool that rejects a candidate should be able to articulate why, in language the candidate and a human reviewer can evaluate.

**Human oversight.** AI systems should operate under meaningful human supervision. Critical choices, especially those affecting people's rights, opportunities, or safety, should include human review instead of being fully automated.

### Algorithmic Bias: How It Happens and Who It Harms

**Algorithmic bias** occurs when an AI system produces systematically unfair outcomes. Bias can enter AI systems at multiple points.

**Biased training data.** AI systems learn from historical data. If that data reflects past discrimination, the AI will reproduce and sometimes amplify that discrimination. For example, if a hiring algorithm is trained on a firm's past hiring choices, and those choices favored men over women, the algorithm will learn to favor men over women.

**Biased design choices.** The people who build AI systems make choices about what data to use, what outcomes to optimize for, and how to measure success. Those choices reflect the perspectives and blind spots of the development team. A team that lacks diversity may fail to consider how the system could affect people unlike themselves.

**Biased deployment context.** Even a well-designed AI system can produce biased outcomes if it is deployed in a context it was not designed for. A facial recognition system trained primarily on lighter-skinned faces will perform poorly on darker-skinned faces, not because the algorithm is intentionally biased, but because the training data was not representative.

The harm from algorithmic bias falls disproportionately on communities that are already marginalized. People of color, women, people with disabilities, and low-income populations are more likely to be affected by biased AI systems in hiring, lending, healthcare, and criminal justice.

### Global AI Approaches

Groups and countries approach AI ethics in different ways. These differences matter because AI systems cross borders. A model trained in one country may be used worldwide and carry its developers' assumptions with it.

**Corporate approaches vary.** A firm may publish principles, model cards, test reports, or audit summaries. Another may treat the same information as proprietary. The ethical test is not whether a policy sounds responsible. It is whether leaders can show how the policy changes design, release, monitoring, and correction choices.

**National frameworks differ.** The [EU AI Act](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai) uses risk categories and phased duties. Prohibited practices and AI literacy duties began to apply in February 2025. Rules for general-purpose AI models began to apply in August 2025. Other duties follow the current Commission timeline. In the United States, the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) remains voluntary and organizes risk work around govern, map, measure, and manage.

**International principles support comparison.** The [OECD AI Principles](https://oecd.ai/en/ai-principles) address human rights, transparency, robustness, and accountability. They were updated in 2024. No single framework removes the need to examine the system's context, evidence, and affected stakeholders.

### Who Is Accountable When AI Causes Harm?

This is one of the hardest questions in AI ethics, and it does not have a settled answer. Consider a case: a hospital uses an AI diagnostic tool that fails to detect cancer in a patient. The patient's diagnosis is delayed, and the cancer spreads. Who is responsible?

* The firm that built the AI tool?
* The hospital that chose to use it?
* The doctor who relied on its recommendation?
* The regulators who approved it (or failed to regulate it)?

The accountability gap in AI is a real and growing problem. When multiple parties are involved in building, deploying, and using an AI system, responsibility can become diffused to the point where nobody is effectively held accountable. This is why clear governance structures matter. Groups that deploy AI need to define, in advance, who is responsible for monitoring the system, responding to errors, and compensating people who are harmed.

Clear governance closes the accountability gap. Before deployment, leaders should name who approves the system, who monitors outcomes, who receives complaints, and who can suspend its use. The [NIST AI RMF Core](https://airc.nist.gov/airmf-resources/airmf/5-sec-core/) treats governance as a cross-cutting function that shapes mapping, measurement, and management.

### The Direct-Verify-Intervene Practice

The **direct-verify-intervene practice** describes three duties for people who use AI. Direct the system toward a clear goal. Verify its results over time. Intervene when an outcome cannot be explained or defended. A group that uses an AI hiring tool is making a governance choice about who receives an opportunity. Fairness, transparency, accountability, explainability, and human oversight turn those duties into questions a team can test.

You may be asked to implement or maintain a system you did not build. Ask what assumptions shaped it, where it has failed, who reviews consequential outputs, and who can stop it. Raising those questions before deployment is part of responsible governance.

### Green Computing and the Environmental Ethics of AI

AI governance is not just about fairness and accountability. It also involves environmental responsibility. Training large AI models requires enormous amounts of computing power, which consumes electricity and generates carbon emissions. Researchers have estimated that training a single large language model can produce as much carbon dioxide as several cars produce over their entire lifetimes.

**Green computing** refers to the practice of designing, manufacturing, using, and disposing of computing resources in environmentally responsible ways. For AI, green computing asks whether the benefits of larger models justify their environmental costs. It also asks whether firms must measure and disclose the carbon footprint of their AI systems.

## 3.5 Summary and Retrieval 💡

### Key Concepts

* **Shareholder theory vs. stakeholder theory** represent two fundamentally different views of corporate responsibility. Shareholder theory says a firm's primary obligation is to its owners. Stakeholder theory says a firm owes obligations to everyone affected by its operations, such as staff, customers, communities, and contractors.

* **Corporate governance** is the system that determines who makes choices within a firm and who holds choice-makers accountable. In technology firms, governance choices carry unusual ethical weight because the products affect millions or billions of people.

* **Corporate social responsibility (CSR)** means that businesses have obligations to society beyond profit. For tech firms, CSR is especially important because of the scale and speed at which their products can cause harm. Practical reasons for CSR include building goodwill, creating consistent behavior, fostering good practices, reducing legal risk, and avoiding bad publicity.

* **Building ethical groups** requires practical tools: Chief Ethics Officers, codes of ethics, social audits, ethics training, and ethical criteria in performance reviews. Policies that exist only on paper do not change behavior.

* **AI ethics is a corporate governance issue.** The five core principles are fairness, transparency, accountability, explainability, and human oversight. Algorithmic bias can enter AI systems through biased training data, biased design choices, or biased deployment contexts. The harm falls disproportionately on already-marginalized communities.

* **Global AI approaches** reflect different priorities. The EU AI Act uses risk tiers and phased duties. The US NIST AI Risk Management Framework offers voluntary guidance, such as a 2024 profile for generative AI. State laws also address parts of AI use. No single global standard exists.

* **AI accountability requires named people and evidence.** Groups should define who approves a system, who monitors it, who receives complaints, and who can suspend it. The NIST AI RMF supports continuous governance, measurement, and risk treatment.

### Case: RecruitIQ and the Bias Discovery

TalentForward is a mid-sized technology firm with 2,000 staff. Two years ago, the firm purchased an AI hiring tool called RecruitIQ from an external vendor. Last month, TalentForward's newly hired Chief Ethics Officer, Dr. Amara Osei, conducted a routine audit of the firm's AI systems. Her findings were troubling:

* RecruitIQ consistently ranked candidates from historically Black colleges and universities (HBCUs) lower than candidates from predominantly white institutions with similar qualifications.
* The tool penalized resumes that included gaps in employment, disproportionately affecting women who had taken parental leave.

Dr. Osei presented her findings to the CEO and the board of directors. The CEO acknowledged the problem but pointed out that RecruitIQ had reduced hiring costs by 30% and that the two-year vendor contract had one year remaining. Breaking the contract early would cost $500,000. Several board members argued that switching systems mid-contract would be too expensive and disruptive. Others argued that continuing to use a biased tool exposed the firm to discrimination lawsuits and reputational damage.
