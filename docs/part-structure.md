# CIS111 Textbook: 12-Chapter Structure (Authoritative)

**Course:** CIS111: Ethics in Information Technology
**Structure:** 12 chapters organized into 4 thematic parts
**Pacing:** Schedule-neutral. Instructors may use the book in 9-, 12-,
14-, or 16-week courses without changing chapter content.
**Prerequisite:** None
**Last revised:** 2026-07-12

---

## Rationale for 12-Chapter Structure

**Why 12 chapters:**

* Mirrors the successful CIS215 textbook chapter structure
* Maps cleanly to the 18 CLOs (1-2 CLOs per chapter)
* Gives each major ethical domain a focused chapter
* Keeps pacing decisions outside the textbook
* Lets instructors pair or separate chapters for their course calendar
* Gives each chapter a complete learning and assessment cycle

**What each chapter contains:**

* Chapter Learning Objectives (MLOs) aligned to specific CLOs
* Instructional content (readings, case studies, multimedia)
* Predict-Run-Explain practice and Quick Checks
* A three-part Skills Lab using the shared 16-point rubric
* Review questions, curated sources, and a bridge to the next chapter

**Note on AI Ethics (2026):** Artificial intelligence ethics is treated as a cross-cutting theme throughout the course. Chapter 3 serves as the anchor chapter for AI governance and accountability frameworks. Every subsequent chapter includes at least one AI touchpoint connecting that chapter's topic to contemporary AI developments. This approach covers AI ethics within the existing 18 CLOs while preparing students for the AI-saturated IT workforce they are entering.

## Part I: Foundations of Ethics (Chapters 1-3)

**Theme:** Building an ethical reasoning toolkit

**Learning arc:** Ethical Theories → Professional Ethics → Corporate Responsibility and AI Governance

### Chapter 1: Ethics and Ethical Theories

**Focus:** Core ethical frameworks and their application to IT

* The distinction between morals, ethics, and laws: and why the differences matter
* Utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics, social contract theory
* How ethical theories apply to technology decisions
* A structured ethical decision-making model: Define the problem → Identify alternatives → Evaluate alternatives → Choose and implement → Evaluate results
* Integrity as a professional and organizational value

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO I: Compare a variety of different ethical theories

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section I (Ethics and Ethical Theories)

---

### Chapter 2: Professional Business Ethics and IT Worker Responsibilities

**Focus:** Ethical judgment in professional IT contexts and the six key relationships IT workers must manage

* Professional ethics vs. personal ethics
* The six relationships IT workers must manage ethically:
  * Relationships with employers (acceptable use, conflicts of interest, trade secrets)
  * Relationships with clients (honest representation, confidentiality)
  * Relationships with suppliers (gifts, favors, and vendor ethics)
  * Relationships with other IT professionals (résumé fraud, whistle-blowing within the profession)
  * Relationships with IT users (protecting user data, honest communication)
  * Relationships with society (environmental impact, digital divide, broader obligations)
* Ethical responsibilities of IT users (not just IT workers)
* Encouraging ethical use of IT resources in organizations
* Complexities of ethical decision-making in business environments

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO II: Analyze professional business ethics and the complexities of making ethical judgments

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section I.b, I.c

---

### Chapter 3: Corporate Governance, CSR, and AI Ethics

**Focus:** Corporate ethics, accountability, and the emerging challenge of governing AI systems

* Responsibilities to shareholders vs. stakeholders: shareholder theory vs. stakeholder theory
* Corporate governance structures and the board's ethical role
* Corporate social responsibility (CSR) in tech companies: why it matters:
  * Gaining community goodwill
  * Creating consistent organizational behavior
  * Fostering good business practices
  * Protecting the organization from legal action
  * Avoiding unfavorable publicity
* How organizations improve ethics in practice: appointing a Chief Ethics Officer, establishing a code of ethics, conducting social audits, requiring ethics training, including ethical criteria in performance appraisals
* The gig economy, contingent workers, and outsourcing ethics: obligations to non-employee workers
* **AI Ethics Anchor:** AI governance as a corporate responsibility issue
  * Key AI ethics principles: fairness, transparency, accountability, explainability, human oversight
  * Algorithmic bias: how bias enters AI systems and who is harmed
  * The global AI landscape: different ethical frameworks from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, xAI, ByteDance/Qwen, DeepSeek: and how national context shapes ethical approach
  * AI governance frameworks: EU AI Act, NIST AI Risk Management Framework, IEEE Ethically Aligned Design
  * Who is accountable when an AI system causes harm?
  * Green computing and the environmental ethics of large-scale AI training

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO III: Examine issues relating to corporate ownership, corporate governance, and responsiveness to corporate stakeholders

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section II.a, II.b

---

**Part I Milestone:** Students can compare ethical theories, apply a structured decision-making model to IT scenarios, identify the ethical obligations of IT workers across six professional relationships, distinguish shareholder from stakeholder perspectives, and explain core AI ethics principles including accountability and algorithmic bias.

## Part II: Digital Rights and Responsibilities (Chapters 4-6)

**Theme:** Individual and organizational accountability in a connected world

**Learning arc:** Social Media → Whistleblowing → Healthcare Privacy

### Chapter 4: Social Media Responsibility

**Focus:** Economic, legal, ethical, and privacy dimensions of social media

* Social media platforms and their ethical obligations to users
* Data collection, targeted advertising, and informed consent
* Content moderation and algorithmic bias
* Social media in the hiring process: ethical and legal considerations
* Cyberabuse, harassment, and cyberstalking on social platforms
* User privacy vs. platform business models
* **AI Touchpoint:** Recommendation algorithms and filter bubbles: how AI-driven curation shapes what users see and believe. Synthetic media (deepfakes) and their impact on trust and public discourse

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO IV: Evaluate how social media responsibility encompasses economic, legal, ethical, and privacy issues

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section II.c

---

### Chapter 5: Whistleblowing and Accountability

**Focus:** Whistleblower protections, fiduciary duty, and private information

* When and how whistleblowing is ethically justified
* Legal protections: Sarbanes-Oxley Act, Dodd-Frank Act
* Fiduciary responsibilities regarding private data: what organizations owe to those whose data they hold
* Balancing loyalty to employers with broader ethical obligations
* Dealing with a whistleblowing situation: practical guidance for IT professionals

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO V: Illustrate whistle-blowing and protection for whistle-blowers
* CLO VII: Interpret fiduciary responsibilities of private information

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section II.e, II.f

---

### Chapter 6: Healthcare IT Ethics

**Focus:** Ethical challenges in healthcare technology

* HIPAA and the foundations of health data privacy
* Mobile health (mHealth) and wireless technology risks
* Clinical IT systems and their ethical implications: Electronic Health Records (EHRs), Clinical Decision Support Systems (CDSS), Computerized Provider Order Entry (CPOE)
* Telemedicine ethics: access, consent, and equity
* Balancing healthcare innovation with patient privacy and safety
* **AI Touchpoint:** AI-assisted diagnostics and clinical decision-making: who is responsible when an AI recommendation leads to patient harm? Algorithmic bias in healthcare AI (e.g., training data that underrepresents certain populations)

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO VI: Analyze the major ethical challenges of operating in a healthcare environment using mobile and wireless technologies

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section II.d

---

**Part II Milestone:** Students can evaluate industry-relevant scenarios involving social media ethics, algorithmic curation, whistleblower protections, healthcare data privacy, and fiduciary responsibilities. Students can identify AI-specific ethical risks in social and healthcare contexts.

---

## Part III: Legal Frameworks and IT Obligations (Chapters 7-9)

**Theme:** Where law meets technology ethics

**Learning arc:** Software Liability → Government Regulation → Intellectual Property

### Chapter 7: Software Liability and Secure Coding

**Focus:** Responsibility for software quality and security

* Software defects and how they cause harm: product liability frameworks applied to software
* Software quality: what it means and why it matters ethically
* Software development methodologies and their ethical dimensions (agile, waterfall, DevOps)
* Capability Maturity Model Integration (CMMI) and quality standards
* Secure coding principles: giving examples of secure vs. insecure practices
* Risk management in software development: cost/benefit analysis in IT ethical decision-making
* Developing safety-critical systems: heightened obligations when failure means serious harm
* **AI Touchpoint:** Who bears liability when an AI system causes harm: the developer, the deploying organization, or the user? Ethical obligations in AI system testing and release decisions

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO VIII: Examine issues with software and software liability
* CLO IX: Apply cost/benefit analysis in information technology ethical decision-making

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section III

---

### Chapter 8: Government Regulation and Self-Regulation

**Focus:** The role of law and professional standards in IT ethics

* Key IT regulations and their scope: GDPR, CCPA, COPPA, HIPAA, and others
* Professional self-regulation and sanctions: how IT professions police themselves
* Corporate and professional social audits
* H-1B visa ethics: obligations to domestic workers and the role of tech companies in visa programs
* Offshore outsourcing ethics: pros, cons, and responsibilities to displaced workers
* Balancing regulatory compliance with innovation
* **AI Touchpoint:** Emerging AI regulation: the EU AI Act (risk-tiered framework), proposed US federal AI legislation, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework. How different regulatory philosophies (precautionary vs. permissive) reflect different ethical values

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO X: Evaluate the influence of government regulations as well as professional self-regulation and sanctions in ethical information technology decision-making

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section IV.a

---

### Chapter 9: Intellectual Property and Fair Use

**Focus:** Protecting and respecting creative and digital works

* The four main types of intellectual property: copyright, patents, trademarks, trade secrets
* Fair use doctrine: what it covers and its limits in digital contexts
* Open source licensing models and the ethics of free software
* Plagiarism, reverse engineering, and competitive intelligence: where ethical lines fall
* Trademark infringement and cybersquatting
* Digital piracy: ethical and legal dimensions
* **AI Touchpoint:** Who owns AI-generated works? Copyright status of AI output. Training data and copyright: did AI companies infringe copyright by training on scraped web content? Current litigation landscape

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO XI: Interpret intellectual property, copyright laws, and fair use doctrine

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section IV.b

---

**Part III Milestone:** Students can analyze software liability scenarios, evaluate regulatory frameworks including AI-specific regulation, interpret intellectual property protections, and apply cost/benefit reasoning to real IT decisions.

---

## Part IV: Security, Privacy, and Professional Practice (Chapters 10-12)

**Theme:** Protecting people and systems, becoming ethical professionals

**Learning arc:** Freedom vs. Harm → Security & Crime → Professional Codes

### Chapter 10: Freedom of Expression, Hate Speech, and Workplace Privacy

**Focus:** Balancing rights with protections in digital environments

* First Amendment rights and their limits in digital contexts
* Hate speech: classification, platform responsibility, and the line between protected speech and harmful content
* Defamation (libel and slander) in online contexts: elements, examples, and consequences
* Strategic Lawsuit Against Public Participation (SLAPP) suits
* Anonymity on the internet: John Doe lawsuits and the right to speak without attribution
* Employee monitoring and surveillance ethics: what employers can legally and ethically do
* Fake news and its ethical implications for platforms and IT professionals
* **AI Touchpoint:** AI-generated deepfakes and synthetic media as defamation and disinformation tools.  AI content moderation: algorithmic classification of hate speech and its accuracy and bias problems.  AI-powered employee surveillance systems

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO XII: Classify hate speech and defamation
* CLO XIII: Assess contemporary issues related to workplace monitoring

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section V.a, V.b

---

### Chapter 11: Cybersecurity, Identity Theft, and Risk Assessment

**Focus:** Security threats, breaches, and organizational response

* The CIA Security Triad: Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability: the foundational framework for security thinking
* Implementing CIA at the organizational, network, application, and end-user levels
* Types of exploits and the current threat landscape: why computer incidents are so prevalent
* Identity theft: methods, scale, prevention, and response
* Federal laws for prosecuting computer attacks
* IT security breaches: case studies in what went wrong and why
* Risk assessment frameworks: evaluating and prioritizing threats
* Formulating security policies: what policies must address and how to enforce them
* Incident response: notification, evidence protection, containment, eradication, follow-up
* Computer forensics: preserving and analyzing digital evidence
* **AI Touchpoint:** AI-powered cyberattacks (spear phishing at scale, social engineering automation, AI-generated malware).  AI in threat detection and security operations centers (SOCs): capabilities and limitations. Deepfake-enabled fraud

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO XIV: Analyze the major identity theft issues
* CLO XV: Evaluate Information Technology security breaches and risk assessments
* CLO XVI: Formulate security policies within Information Technology
* CLO XVIII: Develop prevention, detection, and responses to Information Technology security attacks

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section V.c, V.d, V.e, V.f

---

### Chapter 12: Professional Codes of Ethics and Capstone

**Focus:** Becoming an ethical IT professional and synthesizing course concepts

* ACM Code of Ethics: principles, obligations, and enforcement
* IEEE Code of Ethics: engineering ethics in an IT context
* (ISC)² Code of Ethics: security professional obligations
* Comparing codes across IT disciplines: what they share and where they differ
* IT professional licensing: arguments for and against licensure requirements for IT professionals
* Certification as a form of professional accountability
* Applying ethical frameworks to emerging technologies (AI, IoT, quantum computing, etc.)
* **AI Touchpoint:** ACM and IEEE statements on AI ethics. Emerging AI-specific professional codes. The ethical obligations of IT professionals who build, deploy, or maintain AI systems: and what to do when asked to build something you believe is harmful
* Capstone: Students synthesize course concepts: ethical theories, professional obligations, legal frameworks, and AI ethics: into an original ethical analysis of a real or realistic IT scenario

**CLO Alignment:**

* CLO XVII: Critique professional codes of ethics for Information Technology professionals

**Course Outline Alignment:** Section V.g

---

**Part IV Milestone:** Students can classify harmful speech, assess cybersecurity risks using the CIA triad, formulate security policies, critique professional codes of ethics, identify AI-specific professional obligations, and synthesize course concepts through capstone analysis.

## Bloom's Taxonomy Progression Across Parts

### Part I (Chapters 1-3): Understand, Apply, Analyze

Students explain ethical frameworks, apply them to IT decisions, and
analyze professional and corporate obligations.

### Part II (Chapters 4-6): Understand, Apply, Analyze

Students apply ethical reasoning to social media, whistleblowing, and
healthcare technology.

### Part III (Chapters 7-9): Apply, Analyze, Evaluate

Students analyze liability and legal frameworks, then defend judgments
about regulation, cost, and intellectual property.

### Part IV (Chapters 10-12): Analyze, Evaluate, Create

Students classify speech, evaluate monitoring and security risks,
formulate policies, critique professional codes, and create a capstone
ethical analysis.

## Cross-Chapter Dependencies

**Strict prerequisites:**

* Chapter 2 uses the ethical frameworks from Chapter 1.
* Chapters 3-10 use the decision model from Chapter 1.
* Chapter 11 uses risk and cost reasoning from Chapter 7.
* Chapter 12 synthesizes frameworks and cases from Chapters 1-11.

**Knowledge assumed across all chapters:**

* No prior ethics or IT coursework is required.
* Students can read web sources and create a structured document.
* Each chapter defines new legal and technical terms before use.

## Scheduling Boundary

This book does not prescribe weeks, pairing rules, exams, point totals,
or due dates. Each chapter stands on its own. Course-shell planning may
pair chapters, add assessment weeks, or change pacing without editing
the textbook.
